## Part 1: The Fellow Model — What DT Fellows Actually Do

### Who are DT Fellows?

DT Fellows are early-career professionals (typically 0-3 years experience) placed inside client organizations for 3-6 month engagements. They are not interns doing observation work. They are operating members of the client's team with real responsibilities.

### What does a Fellow do every day?

A Fellow's work has two layers:

**Layer 1 — Execution (visible work):**
- Attending production meetings, tracking daily output, following up on delays
- Coordinating between departments (production, quality, dispatch, procurement)
- Handling operational tasks the team needs done — data entry, vendor calls, report preparation
- Being physically present on the floor, available, responsive

**Layer 2 — Systems building (the actual mandate):**
- Creating SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) for recurring tasks
- Building tracking sheets, dashboards, or workflows that capture what's happening
- Designing accountability structures — who is responsible for what, and how do we know if it got done?
- Documenting processes so that when the Fellow leaves, the system continues working

**The critical distinction:** Layer 1 is necessary. Layer 2 is the job.

A Fellow who only does Layer 1 is helpful but leaves no lasting value — when they leave, everything they were doing stops. A Fellow who builds Layer 2 systems creates organizational capability that persists after they're gone.

Most supervisors evaluate Fellows on Layer 1 (responsiveness, helpfulness, attendance) because that's what they experience day-to-day. **Your job as a psychology intern is to probe past Layer 1 and assess Layer 2 — the systems building and execution improvement that the Fellow is (or isn't) driving.**

### The 8 KPIs — What Fellows Are Actually Supposed to Move

Layer 2 isn't abstract. Every Fellow placement is tied to specific business outcomes the client is paying for. DeepThought tracks 8 KPIs across its engagements. The Fellow's systems building must ultimately move one or more of these:

| KPI | What it measures | Plain-language version |
|-----|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Lead Generation** | New potential customers identified and contacted | "Are we finding enough people to sell to?" |
| **Lead Conversion** | Percentage of leads that become paying customers | "Of the people we talk to, how many actually buy?" |
| **Upselling** | Selling more to existing customers (higher-value products/services) | "Are our current customers buying bigger?" |
| **Cross-selling** | Selling additional products/services to existing customers | "Are our current customers buying more things from us?" |
| **NPS (Net Promoter Score)** | Customer satisfaction and likelihood to recommend | "Would our customers recommend us to someone else?" |
| **PAT (Profit After Tax)** | Bottom-line profitability | "After all costs, are we actually making money?" |
| **TAT (Turnaround Time)** | Time taken to complete a process from start to finish | "How fast do we deliver from the moment an order comes in?" |
| **Quality** | Defect rates, rejection rates, customer complaints, compliance | "Is what we're producing actually good enough?" |

**Not every Fellow works on all 8.** A Fellow in a factory might focus on Quality and TAT. A Fellow in a sales-driven company might focus on Lead Generation and Lead Conversion. But every Fellow should know which KPIs their placement is supposed to impact — and their systems should be designed to move those numbers.

**Why this matters for your assessment:** When a supervisor tells you what the Fellow does every day, you should be mentally asking: *"Which of the 8 KPIs does this activity connect to? Is the Fellow building a system that will move that KPI even after they leave — or are they just personally executing a task that happens to touch the KPI?"*

For example:
- A Fellow who personally calls 20 leads every day is doing Lead Generation (Layer 1). When they leave, the calling stops.
- A Fellow who builds a lead tracking sheet, trains 2 salespeople to use it, and creates a daily follow-up process is building a Lead Generation system (Layer 2). When they leave, the system keeps running.

The supervisor may not describe the Fellow's work in KPI terms. They'll say things like "she helped us reduce complaints" (Quality) or "he made dispatch faster" (TAT). **Your job is to recognize the KPI behind the supervisor's description and then probe whether the Fellow is personally doing the work or building a system that does the work.**

### What the Client Actually Wants

The client — the factory owner, the COO, the founder — is paying DeepThought for a Fellow who does three things:

1. **Learns the domain fast.** The Fellow doesn't come knowing how to make brake rotors or pasteurize milk. They learn from the supervisor and the floor team. The client expects someone smart enough to absorb domain knowledge in weeks, not months.

2. **Takes ownership and drives KPIs.** The client doesn't want an assistant. They want someone who understands the KPI targets, identifies what's blocking them, and builds the systems to hit them — without needing to be told what to do every day.

3. **Manages the change.** This is the hardest part. Building a tracker in Excel is easy. Getting 15 floor workers to actually use it — to change how they've done things for years — is where most Fellows struggle.

This third point deserves its own section.

### Change Management — Where Fellows Typically Fail

A Fellow can build the best production tracker, the most elegant SOP, the smartest dispatch system. If nobody uses it, it doesn't exist.

**Change management** is the process of getting people to adopt a new way of working. In a manufacturing MSME, this is extremely hard because:

- **The people being asked to change are often older, more experienced, and more domain-expert than the Fellow.** A 23-year-old engineering graduate is asking a 45-year-old machine operator with 20 years of experience to fill out a checklist the operator has never needed before. The power dynamic is inverted.

- **There's no formal authority.** The Fellow can't mandate anything. They can only persuade, demonstrate value, and make the new system easier than the old way.

- **People revert to old habits under pressure.** A process works for 2 weeks when things are calm. The moment there's an urgent order or a machine breakdown, everyone drops the new process and goes back to what they know. Sustainable change requires persistence past the first crisis.

**What change management looks like in practice:**

| Struggle (common) | Effective (rare) |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Pins an SOP on the wall and hopes people read it | Walks the cutting master through the SOP personally, step by step, on a real order |
| Builds a tracker and emails it to the team | Sits with each person who needs to fill it in, shows them how, answers their questions, follows up the next day |
| Gets frustrated when people don't use the system | Asks why they're not using it, listens, adjusts the system to fit their workflow instead of forcing the workflow to fit the system |
| Complains to the supervisor that "your team isn't cooperating" | Builds rapport with the team first, earns trust, introduces changes gradually |
| Treats resistance as a people problem | Treats resistance as a design problem — if people resist, the system needs redesigning |

**Why this matters for your assessment:**

When you probe the supervisor, listen for signals about how the Fellow handles interpersonal dynamics on the floor. Questions like:

- "How do the workers respond to [Fellow]? Do they cooperate, or is there friction?"
- "Has [Fellow] tried to introduce a new process or tool? How did the team react?"
- "Has there been a situation where [Fellow] had a disagreement with someone on the team? How did they handle it?"

**Fellows who score 7+ on the rubric almost always have strong change management instincts** — they don't just identify problems and build solutions (which gets you to 8), they get people to adopt the solutions (which gets you to 9-10). Fellows who score 5-6 often have decent technical output but can't get the floor to change behavior.

**A common trap for psychology interns:** You might read a transcript where the supervisor says "the team doesn't listen to her" and score the Fellow low. But probe deeper — is the team resistant because the Fellow is abrasive? Or because the Fellow is introducing a genuinely useful change and the team is defaulting to old habits? The first is a Fellow problem. The second is a change management challenge the Fellow needs support with — very different diagnosis, very different improvement recommendation.

### Why supervisors are unreliable narrators

Supervisors are not lying. They are giving you their genuine experience of the Fellow. But their experience is filtered through:

1. **Recency bias** — they remember the last 2 weeks, not the full tenure
2. **Helpfulness bias** — a Fellow who reduces the supervisor's personal workload gets rated highly, even if they're not building systems
3. **Presence bias** — a Fellow who is physically visible and "always around" gets rated higher than one who spends time on documentation or planning
4. **Halo/horn effect** — one big win or one big mistake colors the entire assessment
5. **Domain ignorance** — the supervisor may not understand what "building systems" means in practice, so they can't evaluate it even if the Fellow is doing it

Your probing questions must work around these biases without making the supervisor feel interrogated or judged.

## Part 2: The Rubric — How to Read It

The rubric is a 1-10 scale organized into 3 bands:

### Need Attention (1-3) — Not yet productive

| Score | Label | Key signal |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 1 | Not Interested | Disengaged. Does not attempt work. |
| 2 | Lacks Discipline | Does work when told, but never initiates. |
| 3 | Motivated but Directionless | Wants to contribute but doesn't know how. Energy without focus. |

**Band 1-3 pattern:** The issue is pre-productivity. The person hasn't found a way to be useful yet. The question is whether it's motivation (1), self-direction (2), or guidance (3).

### Productivity (4-6) — Productive, not yet solving problems

| Score | Label | Key signal |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 4 | Careless and Inconsistent | Produces output but quality is unreliable. |
| 5 | Consistent Performer | Reliable, meets standards, does what's expected. |
| 6 | Reliable and Productive | Completes everything efficiently. The supervisor trusts them. |

**Band 4-6 pattern:** The person is producing output. The question is about quality (4 vs 5) and reliability (5 vs 6). Note: a score of 6 is good — most organizations need reliable executors. But in the DT Fellow model, 6 is not enough — the Fellow's job is to build systems, not just execute tasks.

### Performance (7-10) — Problem identification and systems building

| Score | Label | Key signal |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 7 | Problem Identifier | Spots patterns, flags issues before they escalate. |
| 8 | Problem Solver | Identifies problems AND proposes/implements solutions. |
| 9 | Innovative and Experimental | Builds new tools, processes, models. Creates things that didn't exist before. |
| 10 | Exceptional Performer | Does everything at 9 flawlessly, and others learn from their work. |

**Band 7-10 pattern:** This is where systems building lives. The Fellow who notices "we lose 2 hours every morning because nobody knows which orders to prioritize" is a 7. The Fellow who builds a dispatch priority sheet and gets the team to use it is an 8. The Fellow who builds the sheet, tests different versions, measures the time saved, and documents it as a repeatable process is a 9.

### The critical boundary: 6 vs 7

The single most important scoring decision you'll make is **6 vs 7**. Here's the difference:

- **Score 6:** "He does everything I give him. I don't have to follow up. Very reliable." — The Fellow is an excellent executor of tasks defined by someone else.
- **Score 7:** "She noticed that our rejection rate goes up on Mondays and started tracking why." — The Fellow is identifying patterns the supervisor hadn't asked about.

The difference is **initiative direction.** A 6 takes initiative within their assigned scope. A 7 expands the scope by noticing problems the supervisor hasn't articulated.

## Part 3: What "Systems Building" Looks Like

Since you'll be assessing whether Fellows are building systems, here's what that looks like in practice at a manufacturing MSME:

| Not systems building | Systems building |
|---------------------|-----------------|
| Calling the transporter every day to check delivery status | Building a dispatch tracker that auto-highlights late shipments |
| Reminding the supervisor about pending orders | Creating a daily order priority sheet that the team uses without the Fellow |
| Attending the morning meeting and taking notes | Designing a meeting agenda template that structures what gets discussed |
| Entering production numbers into a spreadsheet | Building a production dashboard that flags when output drops below target |
| Following up with the quality team about test results | Creating a QC checklist that reduces the follow-up cycle |
| Doing whatever the supervisor asks | Identifying what should be asked and proposing it |

**The test:** If the Fellow left tomorrow, would any system they built continue running? If yes, they're building systems. If no, they're just doing tasks.

## Part 4: Interview Traps — What Goes Wrong in Supervisor Calls

These are real patterns we've seen when psychology interns interview supervisors:

### Trap 1: Accepting adjectives as evidence
Supervisor says: "Very hardworking, very sincere, always on time."
Intern writes: Score 6 — reliable and productive.
**Problem:** Adjectives are not behavioral evidence. "Hardworking" could describe a 3 (motivated but directionless) or a 9 (builds systems tirelessly). You need to ask: "Can you tell me about a specific task he completed last week?"

### Trap 2: Confusing helpfulness with performance
Supervisor says: "She handles all my coordination calls. I used to spend 2 hours on the phone with vendors — now she does it."
Intern writes: Score 8 — problem solver.
**Problem:** The Fellow absorbed a task from the supervisor's plate. She didn't build a system. If she leaves, the supervisor goes back to 2 hours of calls. The right score is 5 or 6 — consistent and reliable task execution.

### Trap 3: Penalizing quiet systems work
Supervisor says: "He's not very visible on the floor. Spends a lot of time on the computer."
Intern writes: Score 3 — directionless.
**Problem:** The Fellow might be building the tracking systems that will transform the operation. "Time on the computer" could be data analysis, dashboard building, SOP writing. You need to ask: "What is he working on when he's at the computer? Can you show me something he's built?"

### Trap 4: Letting the supervisor's mood set the score
Supervisor is frustrated about a recent deadline miss. Rates the Fellow poorly overall.
**Problem:** One event is coloring a 3-month tenure. Probe for the full timeline: "Leaving aside last week, how has the overall month been?"

### Trap 5: Not probing for systems
Supervisor says: "Things are running smoothly."
Intern accepts this and moves on.
**Problem:** "Running smoothly" because the Fellow is personally holding everything together (5-6) is very different from "running smoothly" because the Fellow built systems that run on their own (8-9). You need to ask: "If the Fellow took a week off, what would break?"

## Part 5: Probing for Execution, Systems, KPIs, and Change Management

These are the four dimensions you must probe for in every supervisor interview:

### Dimension 1: Driving Execution
- Is the Fellow getting things done on time?
- Are they following up without being reminded?
- Do they take ownership of task completion or wait for instructions?
- Can the supervisor give them a task and forget about it?

**Good probing questions:**
- "When you give [Fellow] a task, how much follow-up do you need to do?"
- "Has there been a time when something got delayed? What happened?"
- "Who initiates the work — do you assign everything, or does [Fellow] pick up tasks on their own?"

### Dimension 2: Building Systems
- Has the Fellow created any document, tool, process, or tracker that didn't exist before?
- Is anyone else using something the Fellow built?
- Would the Fellow's work survive if they left tomorrow?

**Good probing questions:**
- "Has [Fellow] built anything — a sheet, a tracker, a process — that your team uses regularly?"
- "If [Fellow] left next week, what would your team lose? What would keep running?"
- "Has [Fellow] ever come to you with a problem you hadn't noticed, and a suggestion for how to fix it?"
- "Is there anything that works differently now because of something [Fellow] set up?"

### Dimension 3: KPI Impact
- Can you connect the Fellow's work to a specific business outcome?
- Is the Fellow aware of which KPIs they're supposed to move?
- Are the systems they're building designed to improve a measurable number?

**Good probing questions:**
- "Since [Fellow] started, has any number improved — delivery speed, complaints, rejections, sales?"
- "Does [Fellow] track any specific metric or target? Do they show you progress?"
- "If I asked [Fellow] what their main goal for this month is, what would they say?"

### Dimension 4: Change Management and Interpersonal Skills
- How does the Fellow get people to adopt new processes?
- How do floor workers and other team members respond to the Fellow?
- When there's friction or resistance, how does the Fellow handle it?

**Good probing questions:**
- "How do the workers on the floor respond to [Fellow]? Is there anyone who resists or doesn't cooperate?"
- "Has [Fellow] tried to introduce something new — a process, a form, a system? How did the team react?"
- "Has there been a situation where [Fellow] had to handle a disagreement or a difficult person? What happened?"
- "Does [Fellow] adjust their approach when something isn't working with the team, or do they stick with the same method?"

## Part 6: Worked Example — What a Good Analysis Looks Like

Before you start the 3 cases, read this worked example. It shows you what we expect — and what we don't.

### Mini-Case: Priya at Shivam Packaging

**Company:** Shivam Packaging — Rajkot, Gujarat. Makes corrugated boxes for FMCG companies. 90 employees.

**Fellow:** Priya Shah, 3 months in. MBA (HR). Reports to Mr. Bhavesh, Plant Manager.
**Target KPIs:** TAT (reduce production delays), Quality (reduce box defect rates).

**Supervisor transcript (abbreviated):**

> "Priya is good. She comes on time, attends the morning meeting, and takes notes. She made an attendance register for the floor workers — earlier we had no system, people would come late and nobody tracked it. Now there's a proper register. She also follows up with the die-cutting section when we have urgent orders. I don't have to remind her.
>
> One issue — she made a production schedule in Excel last month but it didn't account for machine downtime. We have 2 machines that break down every week. Her schedule assumed 100% uptime so it was never accurate. I told her, she said she'll fix it, but she hasn't updated it yet."

---

### Example: Bad Analysis (what most candidates submit)

**Score: 6 — Reliable and Productive**
"Priya is reliable and productive. The supervisor says she comes on time, takes notes, and follows up without being reminded. She also created an attendance register. This shows she is a consistent performer who completes tasks efficiently."

**Why this is bad:**
- The justification restates the transcript in rubric language. It doesn't analyze.
- It ignores the negative signal entirely (the broken production schedule).
- The score is anchored to positive adjectives, not behavioral evidence.
- No distinction between task execution and systems building.
- No KPI or change management assessment at all.

---

### Example: Good Analysis (what we're looking for)

**Score: 5 — Consistent Performer (with a specific systems building attempt that failed)**

**Justification:**

The transcript shows two layers of Priya's work:

*Task execution (strong):* She attends meetings, takes notes, follows up with die-cutting on urgent orders without reminders. The supervisor says "I don't have to remind her" — this indicates reliable task completion within an assigned scope. She is not initiating these tasks; they are part of a defined routine. This is squarely Level 5-6 behavior.

*Systems building (attempted, not landed):* She created two things — an attendance register and a production schedule. The attendance register is a genuine system: it didn't exist before, it captures data, and it persists without her. This nudges toward Level 7 (problem identification — she noticed attendance wasn't tracked and built a fix).

However, the production schedule failed because it didn't account for machine downtime — a basic operational reality. The supervisor flagged this, and Priya hasn't updated it. This suggests she can identify a problem and attempt a solution (Level 7-8 territory), but her solutions don't yet incorporate floor reality. She's building from the desk, not from the floor.

**Net score: 5, not 6 or 7.** The attendance register alone isn't enough to cross into Level 7 — it's a simple logging system, not a diagnostic tool. The production schedule attempt shows initiative but the failure to update it after feedback keeps her at Level 5. If she had revised the schedule to account for downtime, I'd score 7.

**KPI assessment:**
Target KPIs are TAT and Quality. The production schedule — if it worked — would directly impact TAT by reducing scheduling confusion and machine idle time. But it doesn't work because it ignores downtime, so there's no actual TAT improvement yet. The attendance register could indirectly impact TAT (fewer late arrivals = fewer delays) but the transcript doesn't say whether late arrivals have actually decreased since the register was introduced. No evidence of Quality impact. **Net: the right KPIs are being targeted, but neither system has produced a measurable result yet.**

**Change management assessment:**
The transcript gives almost no information about how Priya interacts with the floor team. We know she follows up with the die-cutting section, but not how — does she call, walk over, send a message? We don't know if the floor workers cooperate with the attendance register or if she had to push them to use it. This is a major gap. **One of the 5 probing questions below targets this specifically.**

**Probing questions I'd ask:**

1. "You mentioned the attendance register — does anyone besides Priya look at the data? Has it changed how you handle late arrivals, or is it just a record?" *(Looking for: whether the system drives action or just collects data. If it drives action — score moves toward 7.)*

2. "The production schedule didn't account for downtime. When you told Priya, what did she say? Did she ask questions about how often machines break down, or just say she'd fix it?" *(Looking for: does she investigate before building, or just accept and move on? Investigation = problem-solving orientation.)*

3. "Has Priya ever pointed out something on the floor that you hadn't noticed — a delay pattern, a recurring problem, a bottleneck?" *(Looking for: Level 7 behavior — independent problem identification, not just task completion.)*

4. "If Priya took a week off, what would stop working? What would keep running on its own?" *(Looking for: the survivability test — systems vs. personal task absorption.)*

5. "How do the floor workers respond to Priya when she follows up on urgent orders? Do they cooperate easily, or does she have to push?" *(Looking for: change management signal — does she have rapport with the floor team? Is there friction? How does she handle it?)*

**Improvement opportunity:**

- **Current behavior:** Priya builds systems (attendance register, production schedule) from her desk based on what she thinks the floor needs. When the system fails to match floor reality, she doesn't iterate — the broken production schedule has stayed broken for weeks.
- **Target behavior (Level 7):** Before building any tool, spend time understanding the floor constraint it needs to handle. After building it, test it for one week, collect feedback, and revise. The production schedule should have had a "Version 2" within days of the supervisor's feedback.
- **One concrete action this week:** Take the production schedule. Sit with the plant manager for 30 minutes. Ask him which machines break down, how often, and how long repairs take. Rebuild the schedule with a downtime buffer. Share it by Friday. This single act — listening, revising, delivering — is the bridge from Level 5 to Level 7.

---

*This is the level of specificity we expect. Notice: every claim traces to a transcript quote. The score considers both positive and negative signals. The KPI assessment connects daily work to business outcomes. The change management assessment flags what's missing. The probing questions target actual gaps — not generic psychology questions. The improvement plan is doable this week.*

## Case 1: Karthik at Veerabhadra Auto Components

### Company Profile
**Veerabhadra Auto Components** — Pune, Maharashtra
Manufacturer of precision-machined brake components for Tier 1 automotive suppliers. 120 employees. Founder-run. Two production shifts. The company is scaling from a single product line (disc brake rotors) to adding drum brake assemblies. The founder, Mr. Suresh Patil, manages production, quality, and procurement himself.

### Fellow Profile
**Karthik Narayanan** — Fellow since 4 months
B.Tech Mechanical, 1 year prior experience at a machine tool company. Placed with Veerabhadra to support the new product line launch and bring structure to daily production planning. Reports to Mr. Patil (Founder/MD). Works first shift (7 AM to 4 PM).
**Target KPIs for this placement:** Quality (reduce rejection rates), TAT (faster production cycles for the new drum brake line).

### Supervisor Transcript — Mr. Suresh Patil (Founder/MD)
*10-minute phone call, Tuesday afternoon. Mr. Patil is on the factory floor and takes the call between checking machine setups.*

> "Karthik? Haan, he is good. Very sincere boy. Comes on time, leaves on time — actually he stays late most days, I don't ask him to. He's always on the floor. He's not one of those people who sits in the office and sends emails. He's hands-on.
>
> What does he do? He helps me with production tracking. Earlier I used to maintain everything in my head — how many pieces came off each machine, what's the rejection rate, what's pending for dispatch. Now Karthik maintains a sheet. Every evening he updates it and sends it to me on WhatsApp. Very useful. I look at it every morning before the shift meeting.
>
> He also handles a lot of the coordination. When we have quality complaints from Tier 1 — they send an email, sometimes call directly — Karthik takes the first call. He notes down the complaint, talks to the QC team, and gives me a summary. Earlier I used to handle all of this myself. Big relief.
>
> The new drum brake line — he's been involved from the beginning. He helped set up the machine layout. He did a study on cycle times and suggested we move the deburring station closer to the CNC machines. Good idea. We did it. Saved maybe 10 minutes per batch in material handling.
>
> Any complaints? No, not really. Sometimes he asks too many questions — like he wants to understand everything before doing it. Sometimes in a factory you just need to do it and learn by doing. But this is a minor thing.
>
> One thing — he doesn't really push back. If I tell him to do something, he does it. Even if it's not the best way. I wish he would tell me sometimes, 'Sir, I think we should do it differently.' But maybe he's still new. He'll get there.
>
> Overall I'm happy. He's become part of the team. The workers on the floor know him. He speaks to them in Marathi — that helps. If you asked them, they would say he's one of us."

## Case 2: Meena at Lakshmi Textiles

### Company Profile
**Lakshmi Textiles** — Tirupur, Tamil Nadu
Manufacturer of knitted garments (T-shirts, polo shirts) for export. 280 employees. Second-generation family business — the founder's son, Arvind, runs day-to-day operations. 4 production lines. High volume, thin margins. The company struggles with order-to-dispatch cycle time — they frequently miss export deadlines, which triggers penalty clauses.

### Fellow Profile
**Meena Krishnamurthy** — Fellow since 3 months
MBA (Operations) from a Tier 2 B-school. No prior industry experience. Placed with Lakshmi Textiles to diagnose the order-to-dispatch delay problem and build tracking systems for production planning. Reports to Mr. Arvind (COO). Works 9 AM to 6 PM.
**Target KPIs for this placement:** TAT (reduce order-to-dispatch cycle time), Quality (reduce rejection rates across production lines).

### Supervisor Transcript — Mr. Arvind Raghunathan (COO)
*10-minute phone call, Thursday morning. Mr. Arvind is at his desk and gives the call his full attention.*

> "Meena. Look, she is smart. No doubt. She understands things quickly. But I have some concerns.
>
> She spends too much time on her laptop. In a garment factory, the action is on the floor — cutting, stitching, finishing, packing. If you're not on the floor, you're not seeing what's happening. I tell her — go to the floor, talk to the line supervisors, see what's stuck. She goes, but after 30 minutes she's back at her desk typing.
>
> She made some Excel sheets. Fine. One is an order tracker — which order is at which stage, what's the expected completion date. Another one tracks rejection percentages by line. She showed it to me last week in a meeting. The data was correct, I'll give her that. She found that Line 3 has 14% rejection compared to 6% average on the other lines. Nobody had quantified this before.
>
> But here's my problem. I showed this to my production manager and he said, 'So? We know Line 3 has issues. The operators are new.' So the data is correct but the people on the floor already know. Meena is telling us what we already know, just in Excel format.
>
> She also wrote something she calls an 'SOP' for the cutting section. Step-by-step process for how to handle a new order — from fabric receiving to pattern matching to cut-plan approval. I looked at it. It's well-written. But nobody uses it. She pinned it on the wall near the cutting master's station. It's still there. Nobody reads it.
>
> I told her — the problem is not that we don't have a process. The problem is that the cutting master gets 5 phone calls during a changeover and loses focus. An SOP on the wall doesn't solve that. She needs to understand the floor reality, not just write documents.
>
> On the positive side — she did something useful with the dispatch team. She started tracking which orders are at risk of missing the ship date and sending me a daily email by 11 AM. Before this, I would find out about delays at 4 PM when the container was supposed to leave. Now I know by 11 AM and can push. Two weeks ago we saved a shipment to Decathlon because of this. That was good.
>
> My worry is that she's building things in Excel that nobody asked for and nobody uses. I need someone who solves problems on the floor, not someone who makes beautiful sheets in the office.
>
> Is she failing? No. She's trying. She's just not connecting her work to what actually matters here. If she learns to do that, she could be very good."

## Case 3: Anil at Prabhat Foods

### Company Profile
**Prabhat Foods** — Nashik, Maharashtra
Manufacturer of dairy products (pasteurized milk, paneer, flavored yogurt) for retail distribution. 180 employees. Founder, Mrs. Sunita Deshpande, is a first-generation entrepreneur who built the company over 15 years. The company is profitable but operationally chaotic — no standard processes, heavy dependence on the founder for all decisions. Quality incidents happen 2-3 times a month (product recalls, customer complaints about shelf life).

### Fellow Profile
**Anil Menon** — Fellow since 5 months
B.Sc Chemistry, 2 years experience as a quality analyst at a pharma company. Placed with Prabhat Foods to set up a quality management system and build standard processes for production and dispatch. Reports to Mrs. Sunita Deshpande (Founder/MD). Works 8 AM to 5 PM.
**Target KPIs for this placement:** Quality (reduce product recalls and complaints), NPS (improve retailer satisfaction), TAT (faster complaint resolution and dispatch coordination).

### Supervisor Transcript — Mrs. Sunita Deshpande (Founder/MD)
*10-minute phone call, Monday evening. Mrs. Deshpande is in her car heading home from the factory.*
