## Factory Glossary — Terms You'll Encounter in the Cases

You don't need manufacturing expertise to do this assignment. But the supervisor transcripts use factory language. Here's what the terms mean:

| Term | What it means | Think of it as... |
|------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Production floor / shop floor** | The physical area where machines run and products are made. Not an office — it's loud, oily, and people are standing. | The "classroom" of a factory. Where the actual work happens. |
| **Shift** | Factories run in 8-hour blocks (morning shift, afternoon shift, night shift). Different workers come for each shift. | Like college has morning and evening batches. |
| **SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)** | A written step-by-step document for how to do a task. Like a recipe — follow these steps in this order. | A detailed instruction manual anyone can follow. |
| **QC / QA (Quality Control / Quality Assurance)** | Checking whether the product meets standards before shipping it. QC catches defects. QA prevents them. | Like proofreading an assignment before submission. |
| **Rejection rate** | The percentage of products that fail quality checks and can't be sold. A 10% rejection rate means 10 out of every 100 units are wasted. | Like a class where 10% of students fail — the system is losing output. |
| **Dispatch** | The process of packing and sending finished products to the customer. "Dispatch delay" = the product is ready but hasn't been shipped yet. | Like courier/delivery — getting the finished thing to the buyer. |
| **Tier 1 supplier** | A company that sells directly to the final brand (e.g., Maruti, Hyundai). Being a Tier 1 supplier means your quality and delivery standards are very strict. | Like submitting work directly to a strict professor vs. through a TA. |
| **Cycle time** | How long it takes to produce one unit from start to finish. Shorter cycle time = more efficient. | Like measuring how long it takes to write one exam answer. |
| **Batch** | A group of products made together in one production run. "Batch record" = the documentation for that specific run. | Like a "set" of identical items made at the same time. |
| **Cold chain** | Temperature-controlled storage and transport (for food, dairy, pharma). If the cold chain "breaks," products spoil. | Like keeping ice cream frozen from factory to store — any gap ruins it. |
| **CNC machine** | Computer-controlled cutting/shaping machine. Very precise. Expensive. | A robot that cuts metal to exact specifications. |
| **Changeover** | Switching a machine from making Product A to Product B. Takes time and causes delays. | Like reformatting your laptop for a different OS — necessary but disruptive. |
| **Production planning / scheduling** | Deciding what to make, in what quantity, in what order, on which machines. The brain of the factory. | Like creating a timetable that optimizes which subjects get which classroom slots. |
| **Line supervisor** | The person in charge of one production line (a row of workers + machines doing one process). | Like a section head or team lead. |
| **Tracker / tracking sheet** | A spreadsheet or document that records what's happening — which orders are pending, what got dispatched, what's delayed. | Like a shared Google Sheet that shows project status. |

## About This Role

This is a Psychology role at DeepThought. You will work on our **performance feedback system** — specifically on the process of helping supervisors assess DT Fellows accurately.

Your job will involve:
- Conducting structured interviews with client supervisors (factory managers, production heads, founders)
- Using a behavioral rubric to convert supervisor impressions into calibrated scores
- Identifying specific, actionable improvement opportunities for each Fellow
- Probing beyond surface impressions to understand what the Fellow is actually doing day-to-day

This is harder than it sounds. Supervisors in manufacturing MSMEs are busy people. They give you 15 minutes. Their default answer to "how is the Fellow doing?" is either "very good" or "not good." Neither tells you anything. Your job is to ask the right questions that surface specific behavioral evidence — and then map that evidence to the rubric.

This assignment tests exactly that.

### How this connects to what you've studied

If you've taken courses in Industrial/Organizational Psychology, Psychometrics, or Counseling, you'll recognize the core skills here — they're just applied in a workplace setting you may not have encountered before:

- **Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales (BARS):** The rubric is a BARS instrument. Each score level is defined by a specific behavioral description, not a vague adjective. Your job is to match observed behavior to the right anchor — the same skill used in performance appraisal systems.
- **Structured interviewing:** The probing questions you design are a structured interview protocol. You're not having a casual chat — you're targeting specific behavioral dimensions (execution, systems building) with questions that elicit evidence, not opinions.
- **Rater bias correction:** The supervisor is the rater. They are subject to halo effect, recency bias, leniency bias, and central tendency. You already know these concepts. Here, you apply them — recognizing when a supervisor's glowing or critical impression is distorting the behavioral reality.
- **Behavioral observation vs. inference:** The fundamental rule: you score what the supervisor describes seeing, not what you interpret or project. "He stays late every day" is an observation. "He is dedicated" is an inference. You score from observations.

**The new skill this assignment adds:** distinguishing between task performance and systems building. This is not a standard I/O concept — it's specific to the DT Fellow model. A Fellow who executes tasks well (Layer 1) looks identical to one who builds organizational systems (Layer 2) unless you ask the right questions. The cases are designed to test whether you can tell the difference. Read Part 1 in `context.md` for the full explanation of Layer 1 vs Layer 2.

### Part A: The Written Analysis (Google Drive)

For **each** of the 3 cases (in `context.md`), submit the following 5 items:

1. **Rubric Score** (1-10) — with a one-paragraph justification citing specific behavioral evidence from the transcript. Not "the Fellow seems productive" but "the supervisor mentioned X, which maps to level Y because Z."

2. **KPI Assessment** (2-3 sentences per case) — which of the 8 KPIs should this Fellow be impacting, based on their placement? Is there evidence in the transcript that any of those KPIs have actually moved? Is the Fellow building a system that will keep moving the KPI after they leave, or are they personally doing the work? *(See the 8 KPIs table in context.md, Part 1.)*

3. **Change Management Assessment** (2-3 sentences per case) — is there evidence in the transcript about how the Fellow handles people — getting teams to adopt new processes, handling resistance, building rapport on the floor? If the transcript doesn't mention this, note that as a gap and include a probing question about it. *(See the Change Management section in context.md, Part 1.)*

4. **5 Probing Questions** — questions you would ask the supervisor in a follow-up call to get the information the transcript is missing. Each question must:
   - Target a specific gap in the transcript (what don't you know yet?)
   - Be phrased in plain language a factory manager would understand (no psychology jargon)
   - Include a brief note on what behavioral signal you're looking for in the answer

5. **Improvement Opportunity Brief** (half page per case) — what should this Fellow work on, framed as:
   - **Current behavior** — what they're doing now (grounded in the transcript)
   - **Target behavior** — what the next rubric level looks like for them specifically
   - **One concrete action** — a specific thing the Fellow could start doing this week

## Evaluation Criteria

1. **Evidence anchoring** — Does every claim trace to a specific supervisor quote? Or are you projecting meaning onto vague statements?
2. **Rubric precision** — Can you distinguish between adjacent levels (e.g., 5 vs 6, 7 vs 8)? The hardest part of behavioral assessment is the boundary — not the extremes.
3. **Probing quality** — Do your follow-up questions target the actual gaps in the transcript? Or are they generic psychology interview questions you could ask anyone?
4. **KPI awareness** — Can you connect the Fellow's daily activities to the business KPIs the placement is supposed to impact? Or do you treat the Fellow's work as isolated from business outcomes?
5. **Change management read** — Can you assess whether the Fellow is getting people to adopt new ways of working? Or do you only evaluate what the Fellow built, not whether anyone uses it?
6. **Improvement specificity** — Is the improvement plan actionable this week? Or is it a vague recommendation like "should take more initiative"?
7. **Bias awareness** — Did you notice when the supervisor's frame (positive or negative) might be distorting the behavioral reality? Did you compensate?
8. **Systems vs tasks distinction** — Can you tell the difference between a Fellow who completes tasks and a Fellow who builds systems? This is the core diagnostic judgment.
