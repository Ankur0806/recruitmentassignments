## Part 1: What Sciensation Roundtables Are

### The Format

A Sciensation Roundtable — what we call a **Scientific Roundtable** — is a 90-minute peer conversation between 5-8 founders and MDs. One KPI topic. No presentations. No slides. No panels. Just a small group of decision-makers working through a question that matters to their businesses.

**Every Saturday.** Tarun moderates using the Socratic method — he doesn't lecture or present. He asks the next question. The thinking comes from the room.

### Who Moderates

**Tarun** — CEO of DeepThought and the moderator of every Roundtable. His background matters because it's part of why MDs take the room seriously:

- **IISER Pune alumnus** — one of India's premier research institutes. Science-first thinking is how he's wired.
- **Worked with Prof. R. Ramanujan** at the Institute of Mathematical Sciences (IMSc), Chennai — a computer scientist and logician. This is where the Socratic, structural approach to problem-breaking comes from.
- **Worked with Dr. Vijay Bhatkar** — the team lead behind PARAM, India's first supercomputer. Bhatkar is a national figure in Indian science and technology.
- **15 years designing execution systems** for Indian manufacturing and services companies — he's sat inside 50+ organizations and seen how they actually run (and where they break).
- **AI practitioner** — not just using AI tools, but building AI-powered products (PDGMS). MDs in R&D-driven companies respect this.
- **Founded Sciensation in 2011** — has been running Socratic dialogues for 15 years. The moderation skill is not improvised; it's practiced across 200+ sessions with jurors ranging from scientists to filmmakers to politicians to global academics.

**How to use this in your invite:** You don't lead with Tarun's credentials — that makes it sound like a speaker event. But if the MD asks "who runs this?" or "what's the format?", Tarun's profile signals that the room is run by someone with intellectual depth, not a community manager or an event organizer. Use it to answer questions, not to pitch.

**Why "Scientific":** The MDs in this room already think scientifically about their product — R&D, validation, experiments, rigour. The Roundtable applies that same scientific thinking to their growth KPIs. "The way you think about your product, applied to your growth" — that's the one-line pitch if you need one.

### How It Works

Each Roundtable is built around one business question tied to a specific KPI:

| Roundtable Topic | The Question Founders Work Through |
|-----------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Lead Generation** | "Where are your leads actually coming from — and which channel has the highest intent?" |
| **Conversion** | "At what stage do your best prospects drop off — and is it the offer, the process, or the person?" |
| **Upselling** | "Which existing customers would buy more if someone was actually working the relationship?" |
| **Cross-selling** | "What's the adjacent offering your customers don't know you have?" |
| **Retention** | "At what point do you lose customers — and is it delivery failure or relationship neglect?" |
| **TAT (Turnaround Time)** | "Where in your value chain does a 2-day delay become a 2-week delay?" |
| **Margins** | "Which product line is actually making money after you account for the hidden costs?" |
| **Compliance** | "What compliance burden is eating operational bandwidth that should be on growth?" |

These rotate. A new topic every two weeks. Over 16 weeks, all 8 are covered, then the cycle repeats.

### What Happens in the Room

Here's a real example of how a session works:

> You're a pharma manufacturer. The person next to you exports textiles. You're both stuck on conversion. Their problem looks nothing like yours — different buyers, different cycle, different team. But four questions in, the blocker is the same: the person who owns that KPI doesn't have a defined process between first meeting and proposal. You'd never have seen that pattern thinking alone.
>
> You walked in with "my conversion is stuck." You walk out with a specific structural question about your pipeline that you hadn't thought to ask.

That's the session. Cross-industry, same KPI, structural patterns that surface because the method goes four questions deep — not because anyone in the room is giving advice.

- The Socratic method means nobody performs. There's no "let me share my success story." The moderator asks a question. Everyone thinks.
- **"Interesting wrong answers score higher than correct ones."** That's the rule. It means the room is safe enough for people to say "here's what I tried and it didn't work" — which is where the real execution insight lives.
- Founders walk out with a reframed understanding of something they thought they already understood

### The Heritage

Sciensation has been running structured dialogues since 2011 — over 15 years. It started with Socratic Dialogues for students on topics like Philosophy of Science, AI (back in 2014), Constitutional Law, and Economics. Jurors have included filmmakers, scientists, politicians, CTOs, and global academics from Finland, USA, Belgium, Spain, and England.

The Roundtable format brings the same method — Socratic questioning, peer thinking, rigorous dialogue — to founders who are building real companies.

One detail that captures the philosophy: **in Sciensation, interesting wrong answers score higher than correct answers.** The point is quality of thinking, not correctness of recall. That same principle drives the Roundtable — the room values original thinking over polished answers.

## Part 2: What Makes This Different

The MDs you're calling get invited to events constantly — conferences, webinars, networking dinners, industry associations, peer forums (YPO, EO, Vistage, TiE). Most of them don't go, or they've stopped going. Here's why this is different, and why that matters for how you invite them.

### vs. Peer Forums (YPO, Vistage, TiE)

This is the comparison the MD will make in their head. Know the difference:

| Peer Forums | Scientific Roundtable |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Broad agenda — bring any topic | One KPI per session. Focused. |
| Same-industry or same-stage groups | Cross-industry by design. The pattern surfaces BECAUSE the industries are different. |
| Experience-sharing: "here's what worked for me" | Structural breakdown: "where exactly does it get stuck, and why?" |
| Quarterly or monthly | Every Saturday |
| Membership + fees | No membership. Show up. 90 minutes. See if it's useful. |

**The key line:** "Stories aren't structure." A peer forum gives you five people's stories. A Scientific Roundtable breaks down the structural pattern underneath all five. Stories are anecdotal — they worked for one person, in one context. Structure transfers.

### vs. Events and Conferences

| What MDs are used to | What a Scientific Roundtable actually is |
|---------------------|------------------------------|
| "Join our exclusive founder event" | 8-12 people in a room. No stage. No audience. Everyone participates. |
| Panels where 5 people give 10-minute speeches | One question. Socratic method. The moderator asks, the room thinks. |
| Networking events where everyone exchanges cards | Nobody is selling anything — not even DeepThought. The room exists for the conversation. |
| Webinars with 200 attendees and a chat box | Small group. You're not an attendee. You're a voice in the conversation. |
| "Thought leadership" events that are product demos | No product demos. No pitches. No sponsor slides. No "and now a word from our partner." |
| Speaker-audience format | No speakers. Everyone is a participant. Tarun moderates, he doesn't present. |

**Why this matters for your invite:** If your invitation sounds like any of the left column, the MD will mentally file you under "another event company" and say no. Your job is to make them hear the right column.

## Part 3: Language Guide

### What to Say

| Approach | Example |
|----------|---------|
| Lead with the question | "There's a conversation happening next Thursday about where conversion actually breaks down — not the marketing funnel version, the version that happens inside a company." |
| Position their expertise | "You've scaled Sai Pharma's API business from 3 clients to 20 — that perspective on lead generation is exactly what the room needs." |
| Describe the room | "It's 8-10 founders, all running companies in the ₹50-500 crore range. No presentations, no panels — just a structured conversation." |
| Name the method | "It's Socratic — the moderator asks a question, everyone works through it. The thinking comes from the room, not from a speaker." |
| Low-pressure ask | "Can I send you the details — who else is in the room, the exact question, and the date? You can decide after you see it." |

### What NOT to Say

| Avoid | Why it kills the invite |
|-------|----------------------|
| "We're organizing an event..." | Sounds like every event company. The MD mentally tunes out. |
| "Exclusive roundtable experience" | Corporate jargon. Nobody talks like this. |
| "Curated peer learning platform" | Sounds like a LinkedIn ad, not a person. |
| "Networking opportunity" | The MD doesn't need to network. They need to think. |
| "Seats are limited, register now" | Creates urgency scarcity — that's a sales tactic, not an invitation. |
| "Our CEO Tarun will be speaking" | Tarun moderates, he doesn't speak. And leading with credentials makes it about DeepThought, not the conversation. |
| Any mention of DeepThought's products | The moment you mention Hire90, PDGMS, consulting, or Fellows, this becomes a sales call. Don't. |
| "I think you'd really benefit from this" | Patronizing. You're inviting a peer, not recommending a course. |
| "Kindly confirm your attendance" | Formal and transactional. You're a person talking to a person. |

### The Tone

Think of it as: you've found a conversation that this specific person would genuinely enjoy and find valuable. You're telling them about it because you think they'd be a great addition to the room. That's it. No ulterior motive. No funnel. No conversion.

The energy is closer to "I read about what you're doing at Sai Pharma — there's a conversation happening next week that you'd find interesting" than "Sir, I'm calling from DeepThought, we're hosting a roundtable and I'd like to invite you."

### The Dinner Test

If your invitation works, here's what the MD tells someone over dinner that evening:

> "I got a call about this Saturday roundtable thing. Scientific roundtable — one growth KPI each week, different industries. This Saturday is conversion. 5-8 MDs, someone moderates with questions, no presentations. A guy from a completely different business had the same blocker I did last time — same structural problem underneath. It's how I think about R&D, applied to my business numbers."

If your invite would lead to that description — you nailed it. If it would lead to "some company called about an event" — you didn't.

## Part 5: The 4 Objections

Record a separate voice note responding to each. Imagine you've just invited Dr. Venkat Rao to the Roundtable, and he says:

---

### Objection 1: "Is this some kind of sales pitch? What's the catch?"

**Why the MD says this:** He's been burned before. "Founder roundtable" events often turn out to be product demos in disguise — a sponsor gets 20 minutes, someone pitches a SaaS tool, or the "free event" leads to follow-up sales calls. He's protecting his time.

**What he actually means:** "I've been tricked by events that promised peer learning and delivered a sales funnel. Prove this is different."

**The reality:** There is no pitch in the Roundtable. No product demo. No sponsor slot. Tarun moderates using the Socratic method — he asks questions, the room thinks. DeepThought's products are never mentioned in the Roundtable. The conversation belongs to the room.

---

### Objection 2: "I don't attend events. Send me the details on email."

**Why the MD says this:** He's polite and busy. "Send me the details" is the standard exit line for any call he doesn't want to extend. It means "I'm not saying no, but I'm definitely not saying yes, and I probably won't read the email."

**What he actually means:** You haven't given him a reason to care yet. He's filing this under "event invitation" — a category he ignores. The email will sit unread.

**The real question:** Can you give him one specific reason — tied to HIS situation — that makes him pause and reconsider? Not a reason to attend "events." A reason to be in THIS specific conversation.

---

### Objection 3: "Who else is attending?"

**Why the MD says this:** This is actually a good sign — he's interested enough to qualify the room. He wants to know if the other people are at his level, in relevant industries, and worth his time. He doesn't want to sit with 20-somethings from startups or with people who'll pitch him their services.

**What he actually means:** "Is this room worth my time? Will I be the smartest person there, or will I learn something?"

**What you can share:** The profile of who typically attends — founders and MDs running companies in the ₹50-500 Cr range, across manufacturing, pharma, tech, services. 8-12 people. No vendors, no consultants, no sponsors in the room. You can mention industries, not names (unless you have permission).

---

### Objection 4: "I'm very busy this month. Maybe next time."

**Why the MD says this:** Could be genuinely busy. Could be a polite no. The "maybe next time" is the softest possible rejection — it keeps the relationship open without committing to anything.

**What he actually means:** Either he's actually overwhelmed (in which case, respect it) or he's not convinced enough to prioritize this (in which case, you haven't landed the value yet).

**Two possible responses:** If he seems genuinely stretched, acknowledge it and offer to reach out for the next one on a specific topic that connects to his world. If it feels like a polite deflection, try one more angle — not pushing the event, but making the topic more specific and relevant. "I understand. The next one is on [topic] in two weeks — given what Sai Pharma is doing with formulations, that one might be even more relevant. Can I reach out closer to the date?"

---

**For all four objections:** You're not overcoming resistance. You're helping someone see past their default "no to events" response and consider whether this specific conversation is different. If they still say no, that's fine. The goal is: did you give them a genuine reason to reconsider, or did you just push harder?
