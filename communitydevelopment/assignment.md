# Assignment: Roundtable Invitation — Sciensation Community
### DeepThought — Community Development Internship

## About DeepThought

DeepThought helps growing Indian companies become better-run organizations through scientific execution — structured thinking applied to daily operations.

**Sciensation** is DeepThought's community arm. It hosts **Scientific Roundtables** — where 5-8 founders and MDs sit together and break down one growth KPI using the Socratic method. No presentations. No panels. No pitching. Just decision-makers in a room, applying scientific thinking to a business question that matters.

Sciensation has been running since 2011 — over 15 years of structured dialogues across science, philosophy, technology, and business. The Roundtable format brings this method to founders who are building real companies.

---

## About This Role

Every Community Development person at DeepThought does one thing: **get the right MDs into the right Roundtable rooms.**

Your day looks like this:
- You receive company research from our business analysts — company profile, MD details, what they're working on, what they're struggling with
- You read the dossier, identify which upcoming Roundtable topic connects to this MD's world, and call them
- You're not selling anything. You're inviting someone to a peer conversation where they belong
- If they push back, you handle it — not by pitching harder, but by helping them see why this room is worth their time
- If they're interested, you confirm the details and follow up

You don't pitch products. You don't talk about DeepThought's services. You don't close deals. You curate rooms. You find the right people for the right conversations.

**This is not a sales role.** The moment it sounds like sales, the MD hangs up. These are busy founders who get 10 vendor calls a day. Your job is to be the one call that isn't a vendor call.

This assignment tests exactly that.

### Part A: The Invitation Voice Note (50%)

You are given a company dossier in `context.md` — **Company A (Sai Pharma Labs)** — and a specific Roundtable topic. Read both carefully. Then record a voice note.

The scenario: you are calling Dr. Venkat Rao, the MD of Sai Pharma Labs. He picked up the phone. Invite him to the upcoming Roundtable.

**Duration:** 60-90 seconds. A phone call, not a speech.

**Language:** Hindi, English, or your natural mix — whatever you'd actually say on a real call. If you normally speak Hinglish on the phone, speak Hinglish. Don't perform. Be yourself.

#### Guidelines — read these carefully

1. **This is an invitation, not a pitch.** You're not selling a product. You're not selling a service. You're telling someone about a conversation happening between their peers, and explaining why their perspective would make it better. The energy is "you belong in this room" — not "we have something for you."

2. **Don't read a script.** We can tell instantly. Bullet points for yourself are fine. Reading full sentences is not.

3. **Lead with the topic, not the event.** Don't start with "we're organizing a Roundtable..." Start with the question the Roundtable is built around. Make the MD think about their own business before you even mention Sciensation.

4. **Connect the topic to the MD's specific world.** The dossier tells you what Sai Pharma is dealing with. Use it. "We're hosting a conversation about conversion" is generic. "You moved from bulk APIs to formulations last year — the conversion question looks completely different when you're selling to a new buyer" is specific.

5. **Position the MD's expertise, not their problem.** You're not diagnosing a gap. You're recognizing what they know. "You've built Sai Pharma's API business from scratch — that's exactly the kind of perspective the room needs" works. "It sounds like you're struggling with conversion" doesn't.

6. **No corporate jargon.** No "exclusive roundtable experience," no "curated peer learning," no "thought leadership platform." If it sounds like an event company's LinkedIn post, don't say it. Talk like a person inviting another person to a conversation.

7. **Have a clear, low-pressure ask.** "Can I send you the details — date, who else is in the room, the exact question we'll work through?" is better than "Would you like to register?"

#### What we're listening for

- **Does this sound like a genuine invitation or an event sales call?** That's the only question. Everything else flows from it.
- Did you make the MD curious about the topic before mentioning the event?
- Did you use the dossier to connect the Roundtable to their specific world?
- Did you position them as someone whose perspective adds value, or as someone who "should attend"?
- Tone — warm, respectful, intellectually curious. Not enthusiastic-telecaller. Not formal-corporate. The tone of someone who genuinely thinks this MD would enjoy the conversation.

#### What kills your application

- Sounding like you're selling something — a product, a service, or an event ticket
- Mentioning DeepThought's products (Hire90, PDGMS, consulting, Fellows) — this is a Sciensation invitation, not a lead-gen call
- Starting with "sir, we're organizing an event and we'd like to invite you" — that's how every event company calls
- Being transactional: "kindly confirm your attendance," "seats are limited," "register now"
- Sounding like a translated LLM output — formal, impersonal, American idiom, no contractions
- A generic invite that could work for any MD at any company

### Part B: Objection Handling Voice Notes (30%)

The MD pushes back. Record a **separate voice note for each** of the 4 objections in `context.md` (Section 5). Respond as if the MD just said this to you on the phone.

**Duration:** 30-45 seconds per objection. Four separate voice notes.

Same rules as Part A: natural language, no script reading, conversational tone.

Read the context below each objection before recording — it explains why the MD says it and what they actually mean.

#### What we're listening for

- Do you acknowledge what they said, or bulldoze past it?
- Do you keep the intellectual tone, or slip into sales mode under pressure?
- Does your response make the MD more curious, or more defensive?
- Do you end with a reason to keep talking, or a dead end?

**Remember: you're not convincing someone to buy something. You're helping someone see why a conversation is worth their time. The energy is completely different.**

### Part C: Pre-Call Prep Note (20%)

You're given a second company dossier in `context.md` — **Company B (Aravind Tooling Systems)** — and a different Roundtable topic. You're calling this MD in 10 minutes.

Write your call plan in **5-7 lines:**
- What's your opening line? (The first thing you'll say after the MD picks up)
- Which specific thing about this MD's background connects to the Roundtable topic?
- How will you position their expertise — what would they bring to the room?
- What's your ask?

This is not a script. It's the notes you'd scribble on a Post-it before dialing. Keep it rough and real.
